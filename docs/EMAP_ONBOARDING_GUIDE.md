# EMAP Onboarding Guide: Looped Event OS

This guide is for new EMAP team members using Looped Event OS for conferences and awards. It is written for experienced event professionals, not technical users.

---

## 1) Platform Overview

### What Looped is

Looped Event OS is an event intelligence platform. It helps teams plan, market, sell, and run events using a structured set of AI-assisted modules.

The platform does **not** replace your judgement. It produces drafts, analysis, and recommendations. Your team reviews, edits, and approves the work before it is used.

### How it works in plain terms

1. You create an event and complete a central brief.
2. You run modules in a set order.
3. You review each output and either approve, edit, or reject it.
4. Approved outputs become context for later modules.
5. The quality of early approvals improves everything downstream.

### The cascade concept

The "cascade" is Looped's structured sequence of modules. Think of it as a pipeline:

- Upstream modules (earlier modules) establish strategy and direction.
- Downstream modules (later modules) turn that strategy into execution assets.
- Nothing important moves forward until a human approves the previous step.

This is why Looped is described as **human-in-the-loop**.

### The six pillars

Looped is organized into six team pillars (workspaces):

1. Marketing
2. Content
3. Commercial Sales
4. Telesales
5. Event Management
6. Portfolio Directors

Each pillar has its own modules, but all pillars share approved event context.

## 2) Getting Started

### Set up your first event

When onboarding, start with one real event that:

- has a clear audience,
- has known commercial goals, and
- has enough historical context (if available).

Avoid starting with your most complex event on day one.

### Complete the brief properly

Your event brief is the foundation for all modules. Include:

- Event name, date, format, and venue
- Sector and market positioning
- Audience segments and priorities
- Revenue goals (tickets, sponsors, tables, entries)
- Brand voice and language rules
- Terms to avoid
- Known risks, constraints, and deadlines
- Previous edition learnings (what worked, what failed)

### Understand the Briefing Hub

The Briefing Hub is your single source of truth for event context.

Anything entered here can be reused automatically across modules. That means:

- consistent language,
- fewer contradictory outputs, and
- less repeated briefing between teams.

Practical rule: if a detail matters later, put it in the brief now.

## 3) Running Your First Modules

### Which modules to run first (and why)

For a first event, run these in sequence:

1. **Industry Intelligence** - establishes market context.
2. **Market Mapping** - defines the competitive and audience landscape.
3. **Persona Builder** - identifies who you are trying to move and why.
4. **Messaging Architecture** - sets your strategic message framework.
5. **Campaign Plan** - converts strategy into a practical plan.

Why this order: every later decision (channels, creative, outreach, sales framing) is stronger when this strategic base is approved first.

### What to expect from early runs

- First outputs are usually 70-90% usable.
- You should expect to refine wording, emphasis, and priorities.
- Quality rises quickly once your team provides clear approvals and edits.

### How long modules take

Exact timing varies by module complexity and input quality. In practice:

- Most individual modules complete in minutes.
- Review and approval usually takes longer than generation.
- A full first strategic chain can be completed in a focused working session.

Plan onboarding with review time, not just run time.

## 4) Reviewing and Approving Outputs

### How to read module outputs

Review in this order:

1. **Accuracy** - Is it true for your market/event?
2. **Relevance** - Is it aimed at the right audience and goal?
3. **Alignment** - Does it match your brand, tone, and positioning?
4. **Actionability** - Can a team member use this immediately?

### What to check before approval

Before approving, confirm:

- No factual errors or unsupported assumptions
- No conflict with your brief or brand rules
- Clear strategic logic from problem to recommendation
- No vague generic statements where specifics are needed

### How to edit before approval

If output is close but not right:

- Edit directly to correct language, focus, or priority.
- Keep your edits clear and decisive (not half-instructions).
- Save the improved version, then approve.

Your approved edit becomes better upstream context for everything after it.

### When to reject and re-run

Reject and re-run when:

- the output is fundamentally off-brief,
- core assumptions are wrong,
- essential context was missing, or
- the logic does not support execution decisions.

When re-running, improve the instruction quality: specify what is wrong, what must change, and what "good" looks like.

## 5) The Cascade

### How approved outputs feed downstream modules

In Looped, approved outputs are treated as trusted context. Later modules read that context and build on it.

Example flow:

`Persona Builder -> Messaging Architecture -> Campaign Plan -> Email / Channel Execution`

### Why approval order matters

If you approve weak strategic outputs early, weak assumptions spread through later modules. Teams then waste time correcting execution assets that were built on the wrong foundation.

If you approve strong strategic outputs early, downstream modules are faster to review and closer to final quality.

### What unlocks what

As a rule:

- Strategic modules unlock planning modules.
- Planning modules unlock execution modules.
- Approved cross-team outputs unlock better collaboration across pillars.

If something is blocked, check whether an upstream dependency is still pending approval.

## 6) Each Pillar

## Marketing Pillar

**What it does:** Defines market strategy, audience logic, and campaign direction.

**Who uses it:** Marketing leads, campaign managers, growth teams.

**Recommended sequence:**
1. Market Mapping
2. Persona Builder
3. Messaging Architecture
4. Campaign Plan
5. Email Sequences / Channel Plans

**Typical outputs:** personas, message pillars, campaign strategy, channel plans, draft campaign assets.

## Content Pillar

**What it does:** Shapes programme narrative, sessions, and speaker-facing content.

**Who uses it:** Conference producers, content directors, editorial teams.

**Recommended sequence:**
1. Industry Intelligence (or use approved strategic context)
2. Programme Narrative
3. Speaker Identification
4. Session Development
5. Agenda Architecture

**Typical outputs:** theme narrative, speaker targets, session concepts, structured agenda drafts, comms-ready narrative assets.

## Commercial Sales Pillar

**What it does:** Builds sponsor strategy and pipeline intelligence.

**Who uses it:** Commercial directors, sponsorship sales teams, account managers.

**Recommended sequence:**
1. Sponsor Prospect Discovery
2. Package Builder
3. Pitch Generator
4. Tailored Proposal Support
5. Renewal / Pipeline Intelligence

**Typical outputs:** target account lists, package structures, pitch framing, proposal drafts, pipeline health insights.

## Telesales Pillar

**What it does:** Supports outbound conversion and call-led campaigns.

**Who uses it:** Telesales managers, delegates sales teams, outbound reps.

**Recommended sequence:**
1. List Segmentation
2. Offer/Script Framing
3. Retell Voice Campaign Setup
4. Call Log Intelligence Review
5. Optimization Loop

**Typical outputs:** prioritized call segments, script guidance, campaign setup inputs, call performance insights, refinement actions.

## Event Management Pillar

**What it does:** Supports delivery quality, awards workflow, and operational readiness.

**Who uses it:** Event managers, awards managers, operations leads.

**Recommended sequence:**
1. Category / Programme Review
2. Judge Discovery (if awards)
3. Judge Pipeline
4. Run-of-Show Support
5. Risk Detection and Post-Event Intelligence

**Typical outputs:** category recommendations, judge longlists, run-of-show planning inputs, risk flags, post-event learning summaries.

## Portfolio Directors Pillar

**What it does:** Provides cross-event strategy and prioritization.

**Who uses it:** Portfolio directors, heads of events, strategic leadership.

**Recommended sequence:**
1. Cross-Event Benchmarking
2. Audience Overlap Analysis
3. Growth Opportunity Detection
4. Community/Portfolio Strategy
5. Spinoff or Format Recommendations

**Typical outputs:** comparative performance views, overlap insights, strategic opportunities, portfolio-level action plans.

## 7) Working With the Weekly Digest

### How to read the digest

Treat the Weekly Digest as your executive monitoring summary. Read it in this order:

1. High-priority findings first
2. What changed since last digest
3. Recommended actions and owners

### What monitoring agents surface

Monitoring agents typically surface:

- movement in performance signals,
- risks that need intervention,
- opportunities worth acting on,
- anomalies that require human validation.

### How to act on findings

For each finding:

1. Confirm relevance for your event.
2. Assign an owner.
3. Define a next action and deadline.
4. Decide whether a module should be re-run with updated context.

Do not let digest items remain "interesting but unowned."

## 8) Retell Voice Campaigns

### How to set up a campaign

1. Define the campaign goal (entry, ticket, table, renewal, etc.).
2. Segment lists clearly (do not mix very different audience intents).
3. Prepare script guidance aligned to approved messaging.
4. Confirm escalation paths for objections and handoff points.
5. Launch with a manageable pilot segment first.

### What the knowledge base contains

The campaign knowledge base should include:

- event basics and offer details,
- audience FAQs,
- objections and approved responses,
- brand language rules,
- clear eligibility and qualification criteria.

### How to monitor calls

Review call logs and summary insights daily during active campaigns:

- Look for repeated objections and unclear messaging.
- Check conversion quality, not just call volume.
- Update scripts and knowledge base quickly based on evidence.

Short feedback loops drive better performance.

## 9) Common Questions

### What if a module fails?

- Check whether required upstream approvals are missing.
- Confirm the brief has all essential context.
- Re-run once after fixing obvious gaps.
- Escalate only if the same issue repeats.

### How do I re-run with better instructions?

Use specific guidance, for example:

- What was wrong in the previous output
- What should be emphasized or removed
- What format you need in the new output
- Any non-negotiable terms or constraints

Specific instructions produce better second runs than generic prompts.

### How do I edit outputs?

Edit directly in plain language. Keep strategic intent clear. Approve only when the edited version is the one you want downstream modules to inherit.

### How do I request more events?

Use your internal Looped/EMAP onboarding process to request additional event workspaces. Include priority order, business goals, and owners for each event so rollout can be staged sensibly.

## 10) Glossary

- **Cascade:** The ordered flow of modules where approved outputs feed the next step.
- **Upstream context:** Earlier approved outputs used to inform later modules.
- **Validation mode:** A review-focused mode used to check quality, alignment, and risk before approval.
- **Generation mode:** A production mode where the module creates new output based on the brief and approved context.
- **Monitoring agent:** An automated watcher that scans signals and surfaces notable findings.
- **has_findings:** A status indicator showing that a monitoring run produced findings requiring human review.

## First 30-Day Adoption Checklist (Recommended)

- Week 1: onboard one event, complete brief, run first strategic module chain.
- Week 2: align pillar owners and standardize approval quality checks.
- Week 3: activate digest review rhythm and assign clear action ownership.
- Week 4: launch a pilot Retell campaign and capture learnings for iteration.

Success comes from disciplined approvals, clear ownership, and fast feedback loops.
